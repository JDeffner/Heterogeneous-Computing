1The assignment, in plain words

Modern computers contain two very different calculating chips: the CPU (the “main brain”, good at everything) and the GPU (the “graphics card”, originally for drawing game frames, now used for any massive computation, including AI). The university course is about using both well.

The exercise sheet asks one deceptively simple question:

The question

The same chip can be 10×, 20×, even 100× slower depending on how you ask it to work. Prove it. Measure exactly when and why.

We answered it with four small experiments, run on a real GPU (an NVIDIA “Tesla T4” that Google lets you borrow for free in the cloud, because this laptop’s GPU is the wrong brand for CUDA, NVIDIA’s programming language for GPUs):

ExperimentQuestion in plain words
A · scalingHow much work do you have to give a GPU before it shows its real speed?
B · divergenceWhat does it cost when threads that must move in lockstep want to do different things?
C · memory patternsDoes it matter in which order you read memory? (Spoiler: enormously.)
D · occupancyHow does a GPU avoid twiddling its thumbs while waiting for memory?

2Two kinds of chips: 2 professors vs. 2,560 students

Both chips do the same elementary thing: arithmetic on numbers, billions of times per second. They differ in how they are organized.

CPU · the professors

A handful of very clever, very fast workers (cores). Each works independently, guesses ahead, keeps notes close by. Give a professor any task and it starts immediately at full speed. Our rented cloud machine had just 2 of them.

GPU · the students

Thousands of simple workers (our T4 has 2,560). Each one is slow and rule-bound, but there are so many. They are organized into 40 “classrooms” (called SMs), each holding squads of students.

Everything in this exercise follows from one consequence: the GPU only wins when you can split your problem into thousands or millions of small, identical jobs. Each tiny job is called a thread (“compute this one number”), and the little program every thread runs is called a kernel.

3The rule of 32: squads that move in lockstep

Here is the strange part, and the heart of Experiment B. GPU students are not free individuals. They are grouped into squads of exactly 32, called warps, and a squad has one shared instruction sheet. All 32 perform the same step at the same moment, like a dance troupe:

step 2:
32 threads (green ticks) = one warp. Every tick performs the identical instruction in every step. This is called SIMT: Single Instruction, Multiple Threads.

Why build it this way? Because sharing one instruction sheet among 32 workers is cheap: you can afford thousands of workers precisely because they do not each need their own conductor.

The catch

What if the shared script contains an “if”? Say half the squad should do exercise A and half exercise B. A squad with one instruction sheet cannot do both at once. It does A first (the B-half stands frozen), then B (the A-half stands frozen). Two exercises → twice the time. This freezing-half-the-squad situation is called warp divergence, and measuring its price is Experiment B.

4How do you time a chip honestly?

Before any results: how we measured, because sloppy measurement produces impressive nonsense.

  • A stopwatch on the GPU itself. When you hand the GPU a job, your program does not wait for it, so timing with an ordinary clock measures the handover (nothing) instead of the work. The GPU can stamp time markers into its own to-do list; we put one marker before the job and one after and let the GPU report the difference.
  • Warm up first, then time. The first run of anything is slow for boring reasons (code being loaded, the chip waking up from power-saving). We run every measurement 3 times unrecorded, then 10 times for real.
  • Take the median. Of the 10 timed runs we report the middle value, so one random hiccup in Google’s cloud cannot distort the result.
  • Check the answers. Every memory experiment re-adds all the numbers it produced and compares against the known correct total. A fast-but-wrong program measures nothing.

The speed units you will see: GFLOP/s = billions of arithmetic operations per second (bigger = faster calculating), and GB/s = gigabytes moved per second (bigger = faster memory access).

5Experiment A · a GPU must be flooded before it flows

Setup: give every thread the same small piece of pure arithmetic (1,024 chained multiply-adds, no memory work worth mentioning), and vary only how many threads we launch: from just 4 up to 67 million. If the GPU were like a CPU, speed per unit of work would not depend on the amount.

It depends enormously:

Shape of the measured curve (the exact plot is blatt02/results/plots/task1_scaling.png). With few jobs, most of the 2,560 students sit idle, and below ~16 jobs the GPU is outright slower than the CPU: its fixed “get everyone started” cost of ~10 microseconds outweighs the entire computation. From about a million jobs it reaches its plateau.
Measured

At n = 4 the GPU managed only 0.86 GFLOP/s, losing to a single CPU core (1.09) and to both cores (2.71); it overtakes the CPU from about 16 jobs. GPU plateau: ~5,173 GFLOP/s, reached from about 1 million threads. The 2-core CPU managed 3.2 GFLOP/s with both cores, and was equally fast at every size, no warm-up scale needed. On this workload the GPU ended up roughly 1,600× faster, a lopsided comparison (2 cores, and the CPU version could not use its own vector tricks), but the shape of the two curves is the honest lesson: the GPU needs a crowd, the CPU does not.

Why the ramp? A classroom (SM) juggles many squads at once and hides small delays by switching between them (more on that in §8). Few threads = few squads = nothing to switch to = idle time. Also, 1,000 jobs spread over 40 classrooms is 25 jobs each, not enough to even fill one squad of 32 per classroom.

The sneaky part: fighting the compiler

A compiler is the program that translates our human-readable code into chip instructions, and it is aggressively clever. If our 32 recipes had been the same steps with different numbers plugged in, it would have noticed, merged them into one recipe with a lookup table, and our experiment would have measured nothing. So each recipe uses genuinely different steps in a different order, and each is marked “do not merge”. The assignment explicitly demands proving the effect is real: at d = 32 the slowdown must exceed roughly 8×. Ours: 19.9×. Real.

Why 20× and not the full 32×? Modern NVIDIA chips (since the “Volta” generation) can interleave the frozen groups a little: while recipe 1’s workers wait for a result, recipe 2’s workers can slip a step in. The disagreement tax stays enormous, just not maximal.

7Experiment C · memory arrives in crates

Now the second half of the sheet: memory. A GPU’s memory (8–16 GB of it) does not hand out single numbers. It delivers fixed-size crates of 32 bytes, that is 8 numbers at a time, whether you want them or not. You ask for one number, a whole crate arrives, the unused rest is thrown away.

The takeaway in one sentence

On a GPU, how you arrange your data is a 10–40× performance decision, before a single line of clever code. Keep numbers that are processed together stored next to each other.

8Experiment D · juggling while the kettle boils

One more puzzle. Fetching a crate from main memory takes an eternity in chip time, hundreds of clock ticks. A professor (CPU) copes with private snack drawers of recently used data (caches) and by guessing ahead. The GPU’s students have tiny drawers. Their trick is different: massive juggling.

Each classroom (SM) can host up to 32 squads at once, even though it can only execute a few at any instant. The moment a squad is stuck waiting for its crate, the classroom instantly switches to another squad that has its data ready:

The takeaway in one sentence

Occupancy is a means, not a score: you need enough resident squads to hide the memory wait, and past that point chasing 100% is pointless.

9So what? The four rules the data paid for

  1. Feed the GPU or leave it alone. Below ~16 jobs the GPU lost to the CPU outright, and below ~1 million parallel jobs it never showed its real speed. Small or unsplittable problems belong on the CPU.
  2. Keep the squads agreeing. Branching code that makes members of a 32-squad follow different paths costs up to the number of distinct paths (we paid 19.9× at 32 paths). The CPU charges nothing for the same disagreement.
  3. Store data in the order you read it. Wasted memory crates cost us up to 38× of bandwidth. Neighboring workers should read neighboring numbers.
  4. Hide the wait with enough squads, then stop. Speed rose until half the squad slots were filled, then the memory itself was the limit.

That is the whole exercise: same chip in every measurement, and factors of 20×, 38×, 1,600× appearing and disappearing purely based on how the work was presented. Which is exactly what the assignment asked us to demonstrate.

10Glossary, in one breath each

TermMeaning
CPUThe computer’s main chip. Few, fast, independent cores. Good at everything.
GPUThe graphics/compute chip. Thousands of simple cores. Only good at huge crowds of identical small jobs.
CoreOne worker inside a chip.
ThreadOne small job (“process this one number”).
KernelThe mini-program every GPU thread runs.
WarpA squad of 32 threads sharing one instruction sheet, moving in lockstep.
Warp divergenceMembers of one warp needing different code paths; the hardware runs the paths one after another.
SM“Streaming Multiprocessor”: one classroom of the GPU, hosts up to 32 warps. Our T4 has 40.
OccupancyHow many of an SM’s warp slots are filled. Fuel for hiding memory waits.

FLOP / GFLOP/sOne arithmetic operation / billions of them per second.
